Introduction

Wikipedia is a beast. A beast which is also many people’s best friend. Haven’t we all been in a situation where we just had to know the name of Napoleon's mother? And the name of the house where she spent her final days? In this case, the link structure of Wikipedia can be quite helpful. Going into the page of Napoleon you might very well find the name of his mother and a link to the page about her life, where you find that she lived at Palazzo Bonaparte in Roma until she died at 85 years of age.

This is the logic which should determine the link structure of Wikipedia. On each page, links should be available to other pages which are related to the first page.

Next

This intuition is what the game Wikispeedia builds on. The game challenges the user to go from page A to page B only through using the links of the respective pages. Stanford University collected data on ~76000 of these games, recording users' navigation through a subset consisting of ~4600 pages of Wikipedia in 2009. This allows us to study how the subset is connected and how users chose to navigate these paths.

Our Dataset

Our dataset is a combination of two datasets - Wikispedia data from Stanford University and Wikipedia data from the website itself. We scraped the network information of Wikipedia from 2010 - 2022 of all existing pages of the Stanford dataset. This resulted in 13 versions of each page which we performed temporal analysis on.

On top of the network data, Wikispedia also provided us the paths that players took while playing the game.

Let's take a first look at the categories of the Wikipedia pages. Science is the most common category, followed by Geography.

What does Wikispedia look like?

We embeded the network generated by Wikispedia into a graph, and then projected it down to 3 dimensions. The closer the pages appear to each other, the closer they are in semantic space. In general, articles of the same category appear close to each other. In particular, if we filter by Science, we can see that there are dense clusters in Physics and Chemistry. Additionally notice that under Science, the Bird cluster is much closer to the Dinosaur and are closer together . In contrast, Geography is more spread out. Wikispedia players apparently find it easier to relate from places to other topics.

This relationship is also present across articles. Try selecting Music and IT. Notice that the IT page closest to the largest Music cluster is Napster, a music "sharing" website popular in 2007.

Links

Let's have a look at the adjacency matrix between categories. In this plot, we observe how often a category is linked by another over the years. For example, we can see that the Geography row is lighter than the Music row. This indicates that more pages link to Geography than pages that link to Music. This makes sense since there are more pages which can be connected to geographical places than Music.

Degree distribution

We can see that the degree distribution is very skewed, with a few pages having a very high number of links, and the majority of pages having a very low number of links. This is a very common phenomenon in real world networks, and is called the power law.

But what does this mean?

However, the in-degree and out-degree are not good predictors of how "important" a page is. A set of obscure pages that is well-connected amongst themselves should not increase their importance.

This is where Pagerank comes in. Pagerank is a metric that is used to measure the centrality of a page in the network. Since our research question involves the development of pages and their incoming and outgoing links, Pagerank is a suitable proxy for how important a page is, and tracking how these Pageranks evolve over time can give us insight into how the network is changing.

The distribution of Pageranks becomes more evenly distributed over time. This means that the pages with very low Pagerank are being developed and becoming more well-linked.
